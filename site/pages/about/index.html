Laboratory infrastructure for global health

OpenELIS Global is a free, open-source Laboratory Information System built for the public good — stewarded by a university, backed by public-health institutions, and shaped by a worldwide community. It runs national laboratory networks that serve millions of people, at zero licensing cost.

Our mission

Strong laboratories are the backbone of public health — but commercial laboratory software is expensive, proprietary, and rarely built for the realities of low- and middle-income settings. OpenELIS exists to change that: enterprise-grade laboratory management that any country can adopt, own, and sustain, without licensing fees or vendor lock-in. When a ministry invests in OpenELIS, the money goes into implementation and local capacity — not into a vendor's balance sheet.

How OpenELIS is sustained

Stewarded by DIGI at UW

The Digital Initiatives Group at the University of Washington leads development and community stewardship, with a lineage in laboratory informatics for global health stretching back roughly two decades.

Publicly backed

Development is supported by the CDC, PEPFAR, and implementing partners — public institutions invested in durable health infrastructure, not a single vendor's roadmap. That's continuity you can build a national program on.

Community-driven

Implementers, developers, and public-health organizations across 26+ countries contribute requirements, translations, and code — in the open, under the Mozilla Public License.

Why open source

Open source is not a compromise here — it's the point. It means no per-user fees and no annual renewals; full transparency into how patient data is handled; the freedom to adapt the system to local workflows and regulations; and the assurance that no vendor can discontinue the software a country's health system depends on. An independent analysis estimates OpenELIS embodies hundreds of person-years of engineering — available to every ministry, for free. See the full evidence and value case.
